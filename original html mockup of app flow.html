HOW TO USE THE SESSION PLANS

At HERDMind®, we believe that each equine facilitated session is a unique unfolding between the horses and humans present. We truly believe that "It's not about the activity" - the creator of HERDMind, Dr. Veronica Lac, even wrote a book all about that! So, why have we developed this session plan generator?

We recognize that even the most seasoned practitioner might need some help to think creatively in sessions. We also hear from new practitioners asking for help to get them started with ideas for different client populations.

All of the session plans that our generator creates are designed using The HERD Institute Model™ as a foundation, so you can adapt, flex, and modify each session however you want to meet the needs of your specific client population. They are tried and tested sessions curated by our faculty members and not AI generated. All sessions incorporate our equine partners in a compassionate and relational way, and encourages practitioners to allow for freedom, choice, and agency of their equine friends.

These sessions are NOT designed for you to follow step-by-step as tasks to complete. Instead, they offer a broad framework of ideas for you to take and make them your own.

Each session begins by assuming that you have already covered the basics of safety and confidentiality in your session.

Have some ideas of your own? Feel free to share a session plan with us in our discussion forum!

Woven Identity

Specific Focus: Identity & Belonging

Woven Identity

Session Focus:

Identity & Belonging

Materials Needed:

Multicolored ribbons, markers/pens

This session supports exploration of identity and belonging. Participants use multicolored ribbons to represent parts of themselves, their communities, and their connections while spending time with horses.

Session Objectives

1. Choose and express parts of identity using simple symbolic materials.

2. Experience self-directed movement and choice of space as affirming ways to be present.

Session Details

The session begins with participants choosing from a variety of multicolored ribbons. They may hold, wear, or tie their ribbons together to create a larger item.

The facilitator introduces the idea of identity as layered and changing, and as something that belongs to each person. Participants then carry their ribbons into the pasture or arena where horses are at liberty.

Participants move through the space in their own way, choosing to connect with horses, other participants, or remain on their own. The facilitator invites reflection on comfort, expression, and being seen. Reflective prompts may include, "How might your ribbons help you to connect with others - horses and humans?"

Horses may approach at their own pace. Participants notice moments of connection/disconnection and reflect on how these experiences relate to feeling included or excluded in their lives.

The session ends in circle, where participants may share one thing they learned about themselves in their interactions with the horses.

Equine Welfare Considerations

  • Include horses accustomed to fluttering ribbons.
  • Make sure horses don't ingest the ribbons.

Facilitator Notes

Avoid assumptions about gender/cultural expression, or relationship dynamics. Name and normalize the wide range of comfort with visibility, choice, and shared space.

Variations

Variation 1:

Participants are invited to pick a ribbon and write on the ribbon what support they need to feel a sense of belonging. The group then creates a lead rope with their ribbons and leads a horse through a series of cones. How do the supports they need show up in the process? What is the horse teaching them about support? Paying attention to how the horses respond to being led, participants can choose to allow the horse off the ribbon lead. What choices does the horse have in the moment? Where do they want to go? What happens if they don't go through the cones? How can we remain connected if we don't want the same thing?

Variation 2:

Participants are invited to pick a ribbon and write on the ribbon what support they need to feel a sense of belonging. The group then weaves the ribbons together to create something long enough to form a circle in the pasture. Invite participants to stand in the circle once complete and explore what being inside/outside the circle feels like. Is the space big enough? What about the horses? How do we invite them in without pushing others out? Who wants to be in or out? What does that mean for them and how does that show up in their lives? What if the horses don't want to come into the circle? What does that bring up?

Variation 3:

Participants collaborate to braid or weave their ribbons together to clip onto the horses tails. Aim to slow down the process to be intentional about how to introduce the ribbons to the horses. Option to have horses choose their preferred ribbon - How can we tell what they might want? What is it like to see the horses holding the support?
